# RHEL 9 Migration Proposal — 16-Slide Blueprint

## Slide 1: Cover
CentOS 7 to RHEL 9: Enterprise Standardization for a Restricted-Network, Public-Sector Environment

## Slide 2: Executive Summary
- CentOS 7 is end of life across the ~200-server estate; no further upstream security patches are available.
- We propose migrating to RHEL 9 (exact minor release to be confirmed at kickoff `[unverified]`) as the single enterprise standard, executed as a phased migration inside the existing disconnected network.
- The RFP contains one unresolved requirement conflict (offline operation vs. real-time cloud insight) and two undefined scope points (certification target, HA baseline). Each is addressed directly in this blueprint rather than assumed away.

## Slide 3: Customer Challenges
- Roughly 200 servers on an unsupported OS, increasing security and audit risk.
- No vendor accountability for patches or defect resolution today.
- Build drift across the estate raises both operational and compliance overhead.
- Audience: program leadership (risk/cost) and infrastructure/security engineers (technical validation).

## Slide 4: Proposed Solution
- Standardize the full estate on RHEL 9 as the enterprise Linux baseline.
- Move from many inconsistent builds to a single golden image and a single subscription/patch source.
- Keep the migrated environment operating entirely inside the current disconnected network boundary.

## Slide 5: Comparison with Community Rebuild Distributions
- Rebuild distributions depend on upstream re-publishing timelines, which can introduce patch-availability lag relative to a vendor release train `[unverified — confirm current comparator SLA if one is published]`.
- They typically lack a single, contracted vendor support and escalation relationship.
- We raise this comparison early because it is central to the "why RHEL 9 specifically" question; criteria used are limited to vendor support/escalation, lifecycle predictability, and certification-ecosystem access, not general superiority claims.

## Slide 6: Migration Approach & Technical Architecture
- Full application/dependency inventory precedes any migration wave.
- Leapp-based in-place upgrade for eligible hosts; rebuild-and-restore for the rest `[unverified — confirm current supported Leapp source/target versions against this estate]`.
- Migration sequenced in waves by operational criticality, starting with a pilot group.
- Entitlement/content delivery model appropriate to the disconnected network, exact mechanism confirmed during discovery.

## Slide 7: RHEL 9 Lifecycle & Support Benefits
- RHEL 9 follows a defined, vendor-published multi-phase support lifecycle rather than CentOS 7's community end-of-life model `[phase end dates unverified — confirm against the current official Red Hat lifecycle page]`.
- This gives the customer a contracted patch and support commitment for planning purposes.
- Recommend the confirmed dates be documented directly in the SOW.

## Slide 8: Security & Compliance Approach
- The RFP requests "security certification coverage" without naming a standard. We treat this as an open scope item to confirm (candidate baselines include an OpenSCAP profile, STIG, or an organization-specific standard) rather than presuming one.
- Independent of that decision, the baseline build applies SELinux enforcing, an approved crypto-policy level, and centralized audit logging; FIPS mode only if formally required and confirmed `[unverified]`.
- Availability of these controls is not the same as certification; formal mapping to the confirmed standard is a required next step.

## Slide 9: Patch Management in a Disconnected Network
- All patching is served from a locally hosted repository mirror; no host requires outbound internet access.
- This is in direct tension with the RFP's separate request for "real-time cloud-based operational insights" — both cannot be satisfied literally and simultaneously.
- Recommended handling: the environment stays fully offline by default; any cloud-insight capability is scoped as a distinct, explicitly reviewed exception (e.g., a scheduled one-way export) subject to the customer's security authority approval, not a default part of this migration.

## Slide 10: Operational Automation & Standard Build Process
- Kickstart/Image Builder produces one golden image applied to every new or rebuilt server.
- Ansible content and RHEL system roles enforce configuration and detect drift.
- Offline operation means vendor-hosted telemetry/insight services are not available by default; local monitoring covers that gap, which is a known trade-off rather than an unstated limitation.

## Slide 11: Business Value
- Removes the compliance exposure of running an unsupported OS in a public-sector environment.
- Converts fragmented, hard-to-audit builds into one reportable standard.
- Establishes a contracted support relationship in place of informal community reliance.

## Slide 12: Operational Value & Service Continuity
- The "zero service interruption" requirement is not paired with a described HA capability for the affected workloads — the second open scope item.
- Where load-balanced or active/active HA already exists, rolling per-node migration can approach zero visible impact.
- Where it does not, a maintenance window is required per workload, and this should be confirmed before any per-application continuity commitment is made.

## Slide 13: Risk & Mitigation
- Compatibility risk: addressed via pre-migration testing per wave in a non-production environment.
- Cutover/rollback risk: addressed via pre-wave backups and a documented rollback procedure.
- Open scope items (certification target, HA baseline): tracked explicitly as discovery-phase deliverables, not silent assumptions.
- Schedule risk: contingency time built into each wave.

## Slide 14: Support Model & Escalation
- Three-tier escalation: local operations (Tier 1) → certified partner or vendor Tier 2 → vendor backline (Tier 3).
- Response-time targets per severity to be defined in the support agreement `[unverified — not yet set]`.
- One named technical contact recommended for the engagement.

## Slide 15: Migration Roadmap & Schedule
- Weeks 1-3: Discovery, dependency mapping, and the certification/HA confirmation workshop.
- Weeks 4-14: Wave-based migration of the remaining estate.
- Weeks 15-17: Stabilization, validation, and CentOS 7 decommission.

## Slide 16: Success Criteria, Conclusion & Next Steps
- Success criteria: full estate on RHEL 9, confirmed certification baseline validated, signed support agreement with defined SLAs, no unresolved critical post-migration findings.
- Conclusion: this proposal re-establishes vendor-backed lifecycle and support coverage without changing the estate's disconnected operating posture; the certification target and HA baseline are tracked as discovery-phase confirmations, not blockers.
- Next steps: schedule the certification/HA confirmation workshop, approve the pilot wave, finalize the support agreement.
