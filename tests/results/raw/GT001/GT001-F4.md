## Slide 2 — Executive Summary
- The ~200-server CentOS 7 estate is past end of life and no longer receives upstream security patches.
- We propose standardizing on RHEL 9 (minor release to be confirmed at kickoff; treat as `[unverified]` until fixed) via a phased, wave-based migration that stays fully within the current disconnected network.
- Two RFP scope points are explicitly unresolved and are treated as discovery-phase confirmations rather than assumptions: the named security certification, and the HA capability behind the "zero interruption" requirement. A third item — offline operation versus real-time cloud insight — is a direct requirement conflict, addressed in Slide 8.

## Slide 3 — Customer Challenges
- ~200 production servers running an OS with no further vendor or community security patches.
- No enforceable support SLA under the current model.
- Inconsistent historical builds increase patch, audit, and incident-response effort.
- Audience: agency IT/program leadership (cost and risk) and infrastructure/security engineering (technical validation).

## Slide 4 — Proposed Solution
- Standardize the estate on RHEL 9 as the single enterprise Linux platform.
- Replace inconsistent builds with one golden image and one subscription/entitlement source.
- Preserve the disconnected operating model through migration and steady-state operations.

## Slide 5 — Migration Approach & Technical Architecture
- Full inventory and dependency mapping across all in-scope servers before wave planning begins.
- Leapp-based in-place upgrade where source/target versions are supported; clean RHEL 9 build-and-restore otherwise `[unverified — confirm current Leapp support matrix against the estate's actual CentOS 7 point releases]`.
- Migration in ordered waves (pilot, then production waves by criticality).
- Repository/content mirroring appliance (e.g., a Satellite-style capsule or equivalent local mirror) serving as the sole patch and content source for the disconnected network; exact platform to be confirmed against the customer's current tooling.

## Slide 6 — RHEL 9 Lifecycle & Support Benefits
- RHEL 9 carries a defined, vendor-published multi-phase lifecycle (full support, then extended maintenance phases) in place of CentOS 7's community end-of-life status `[exact phase end dates unverified — confirm against the current official Red Hat lifecycle documentation before the support agreement is signed]`.
- Delivers a contractual patch and errata commitment for planning purposes.
- Recommendation: record confirmed dates directly in the SOW, not as an informal reference.

## Slide 7 — Security & Compliance Approach
- The RFP asks for "security certification coverage" without specifying which standard; we do not default to any particular certification and instead flag this as a required confirmation (e.g., an OpenSCAP profile, STIG, or an internal organizational baseline).
- Baseline hardening applied regardless of the final target: SELinux enforcing, an approved crypto-policy setting, centralized audit logging; FIPS mode only if formally required and confirmed with the customer `[unverified]`.
- We state explicitly that these mechanisms being present does not equal certification — control mapping against the confirmed standard is a distinct, required activity.

## Slide 8 — Patch Management in a Disconnected Network (and the Offline/Real-Time Conflict)
- All patch content is delivered from the internal mirror described in Slide 5; hosts never require direct internet access.
- The RFP separately asks for "real-time cloud-based operational insights," which conflicts with the stated "fully offline operation" requirement — both cannot be literally true at once.
- Recommended resolution: the environment remains fully offline by default for all operations. Any cloud-insight capability is treated as an optional, separately reviewed exception — for example, a scheduled and sanitized one-way export on an approved cadence — implemented only with explicit sign-off from the customer's security authority, not folded silently into the default architecture.

## Slide 9 — Operational Automation & Standard Build Process
- Kickstart/Image Builder-based golden image applied to every new or rebuilt server.
- RHEL system roles and configuration management enforce the baseline and detect drift.
- Because connected vendor telemetry/insight services are unavailable offline, health and drift visibility rely on the local monitoring/logging stack — a known and disclosed trade-off of the offline posture.

## Slide 10 — Business Value
- Eliminates the compliance and audit exposure of an unsupported OS.
- Converts a fragmented build history into a single, reportable enterprise standard.
- Replaces informal community reliance with a contracted vendor support relationship.

## Slide 11 — Operational Value & Service Continuity
- "Zero service interruption" is requested without a stated HA capability for affected workloads — the second open scope item.
- For workloads with existing load-balanced or active/active HA, rolling per-node migration can approach zero visible impact.
- For workloads without HA today, a scheduled maintenance window is required; we recommend confirming HA status per workload before committing to any per-application continuity claim.

## Slide 12 — Risk & Mitigation
- Application compatibility risk: mitigated through pre-migration testing in staging, per wave.
- Cutover/rollback risk: mitigated via pre-wave backups/snapshots and a documented rollback procedure.
- Undefined certification and HA scope: tracked as explicit discovery-phase deliverables rather than silent assumptions.
- Schedule risk: contingency time built into each migration phase.

## Slide 13 — Comparison with Community Rebuild Distributions
- Rebuild distributions depend on upstream re-publishing cadence, which can lag a vendor release train `[unverified — confirm current comparator's published patch SLA, if any]`.
- They generally do not carry a contracted vendor support and escalation relationship.
- Comparison is limited to vendor support/escalation, lifecycle predictability, and certification-ecosystem access — the criteria most relevant to a public-sector operator with formal accountability requirements — rather than a general quality claim.

## Slide 14 — Support Model & Escalation
- Tiered escalation: local Tier 1 operations → certified partner/vendor Tier 2 → vendor backline Tier 3.
- Severity-based response targets to be defined in the support agreement `[unverified — not yet set]`.
- One named technical point of contact recommended for the duration of the engagement.

## Slide 15 — Migration Roadmap & Schedule
- Weeks 1-4: Discovery, dependency mapping, certification/HA confirmation workshop, pilot wave.
- Weeks 5-16: Wave-based migration of the remaining estate, sequenced by criticality and confirmed HA status.
- Weeks 17-19: Stabilization, validation, and CentOS 7 decommission.

## Slide 16 — Success Criteria, Conclusion & Next Steps
- Success criteria: 100% of the estate migrated to RHEL 9, confirmed certification baseline validated, signed support agreement with defined SLAs, no unresolved critical findings post-migration.
- Conclusion: this proposal restores vendor-backed lifecycle and support coverage while preserving the disconnected operating model; the certification target and HA status are tracked as pre-planning confirmations rather than blockers to starting the engagement.
- Next steps: schedule the certification/HA confirmation workshop, approve the pilot wave, finalize the support agreement.
