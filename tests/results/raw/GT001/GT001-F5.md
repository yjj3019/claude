# RHEL 9 Standardization Proposal — 16-Slide Blueprint

## 1 | Cover
From CentOS 7 to RHEL 9: A Supported Enterprise Standard for [Public-Sector Organization]

## 2 | Executive Summary
- CentOS 7 is end of life across the estate (~200 servers); no further upstream security patches are being published.
- This proposal standardizes the estate on RHEL 9 (exact minor to be confirmed at kickoff `[unverified]`), migrated in controlled waves while remaining fully inside the current disconnected network.
- Three RFP items require explicit handling rather than assumption: an unnamed security certification target, an unstated HA baseline behind "zero interruption," and a direct conflict between "fully offline operation" and "real-time cloud-based operational insights." Each is addressed in the relevant slide below.

## 3 | Customer Challenges
- ~200 servers on an unsupported OS with growing security and audit exposure.
- No enforceable vendor SLA for the current platform.
- Inconsistent build history across the estate increases patch and compliance overhead.
- Audience: program/IT leadership (business risk) and infrastructure/security engineers (technical evaluation).

## 4 | Proposed Solution
- Standardize on RHEL 9 as the enterprise Linux baseline for all in-scope servers.
- Consolidate to a single golden build and a single subscription/patch source.
- Retain the existing disconnected-network operating model throughout and after migration.

## 5 | Migration Approach & Technical Architecture
- Application/dependency inventory across the full estate ahead of any wave planning.
- Leapp in-place upgrade where supported; rebuild-and-restore where not `[unverified — confirm current Leapp source/target support matrix against this estate]`.
- Wave-based rollout ordered by operational criticality, beginning with a pilot group.
- Local repository mirror as the sole entitlement/content source, sized and configured for the disconnected network; exact platform confirmed during discovery.

## 6 | RHEL 9 Lifecycle & Support Benefits
- RHEL 9 has a defined, vendor-published multi-phase support lifecycle, unlike CentOS 7's community end-of-life state `[phase end dates unverified — confirm against the current official lifecycle page before the support agreement is finalized]`.
- Gives the customer a contracted patch/errata commitment for planning purposes.
- Recommend capturing confirmed dates directly in the SOW.

## 7 | Security & Compliance Approach
- The RFP does not name the required "security certification"; we treat the target standard as an open confirmation item (e.g., OpenSCAP profile, STIG, or an internal organizational baseline) rather than guessing.
- Baseline build includes SELinux enforcing, an approved crypto-policy level, and centralized audit logging regardless of the final target; FIPS mode only if formally required and confirmed `[unverified]`.
- Having these mechanisms available is not equivalent to certification — formal control mapping against the confirmed standard follows as a distinct activity.

## 8 | Patch Management vs. Real-Time Insight (the Offline Conflict)
- Patch content is served entirely from the internal mirror in Slide 5; no host reaches the internet directly.
- This conflicts with the RFP's separate ask for "real-time cloud-based operational insights" — both cannot be true simultaneously as literally written.
- Recommended path: keep operations fully offline by default; treat cloud-insight capability as an optional, separately security-reviewed exception (for example, a scheduled sanitized export) enabled only with explicit customer security-authority approval — not assumed into the baseline design.

## 9 | Operational Automation & Standard Build Process
- Kickstart/Image Builder-driven golden image for every new or rebuilt server.
- RHEL system roles and configuration management maintain and re-assert the baseline, with drift detection.
- Offline operation means connected vendor telemetry/insight services are unavailable by default; the local monitoring/logging stack covers this gap — a disclosed trade-off, not a hidden limitation.

## 10 | Business Value
- Removes the compliance liability of an unsupported OS in a public-sector context.
- Replaces fragmented, hard-to-audit builds with one standardized, reportable baseline.
- Establishes a contracted vendor support relationship in place of informal community reliance.

## 11 | Operational Value & Service Continuity
- "Zero service interruption" is requested without a described HA capability for the affected workloads.
- Where HA already exists (load-balanced/active-active), rolling per-node migration can approach zero visible impact.
- Where it does not, a maintenance window is required per workload; recommend confirming HA status before making any per-application continuity commitment.

## 12 | Risk & Mitigation
- Compatibility risk: mitigated via pre-migration staging tests per wave.
- Cutover/rollback risk: mitigated via pre-wave backups and a documented rollback path.
- Open scope (certification, HA): tracked explicitly as discovery deliverables, not silent assumptions.
- Schedule risk: contingency time reserved in each wave.

## 13 | Comparison with Community Rebuild Distributions
- Rebuild distributions depend on upstream re-publishing cadence, which can trail a vendor release train `[unverified — confirm current comparator's published SLA, if any]`.
- They generally lack a contracted, single-vendor support and escalation path.
- Comparison is scoped to vendor support/escalation, lifecycle predictability, and certification-ecosystem access — criteria relevant to a public-sector operator's accountability requirements — not a general superiority claim.

## 14 | Support Model & Escalation
- Tiered escalation: local Tier 1 → certified partner/vendor Tier 2 → vendor backline Tier 3.
- Response-time targets by severity to be defined in the support agreement `[unverified — not yet set]`.
- Single named technical contact recommended for the engagement.

## 15 | Migration Roadmap & Schedule
- Weeks 1-3: Discovery, dependency mapping, certification/HA confirmation session.
- Weeks 4-15: Wave-based migration of the remaining estate.
- Weeks 16-18: Stabilization, validation, CentOS 7 decommission.

## 16 | Success Criteria, Conclusion & Next Steps
- Success criteria: full estate on RHEL 9, confirmed certification baseline validated, signed support agreement with defined SLAs, no unresolved critical post-migration findings.
- Conclusion: this proposal restores vendor-backed lifecycle and support coverage without altering the estate's disconnected posture; certification target, HA baseline, and the offline/insight conflict are resolved with explicit, confirmable positions rather than assumptions.
- Next steps: hold the certification/HA confirmation session, approve the pilot wave, finalize the support agreement.
