# RHEL 9 Enterprise Standardization Proposal — 16-Slide Blueprint

## Slide 1 — Cover
Enterprise Linux Standardization: CentOS 7 to RHEL 9
Proposal for [Public-Sector Organization] — Restricted Network Environment

## Slide 2 — Executive Summary
- CentOS 7 has passed end of life; the ~200-server estate is now running on an unsupported OS.
- We propose standardizing the estate on RHEL 9 (assumed RHEL 9.4 as the current minor release; confirm exact target minor at contract signature `[unverified]`) with a phased, wave-based migration.
- This restores vendor-backed patch and lifecycle coverage, reduces operational inconsistency, and keeps the environment fully within its disconnected network boundary.
- Two RFP items require confirmation before final design lock: the specific security certification target, and current high-availability capability of in-scope workloads. Both are addressed as open items below, not blocking assumptions.

## Slide 3 — Customer Challenges
- ~200 servers on CentOS 7, which no longer receives upstream security patches.
- No vendor support relationship for the current OS base; incident resolution relies on community resources.
- Inconsistent server build history increases both patch and audit overhead.
- Audience: public-sector IT leadership (business case) and infrastructure/security engineering (technical evaluation).

## Slide 4 — Proposed Solution Overview
- Adopt RHEL 9 as the single standardized enterprise Linux platform for the estate.
- Replace ad hoc builds with one golden image, one subscription model, one patch source.
- Migration and ongoing operations designed to run entirely inside the existing disconnected network boundary.

## Slide 5 — Migration Approach & Technical Architecture
- Discovery phase: full application and dependency inventory across all ~200 servers.
- In-place upgrade path (Leapp) for eligible hosts; clean RHEL 9 build-and-restore for hosts that do not meet Leapp's supported upgrade criteria `[unverified — confirm current Leapp-supported source/target matrix for this estate]`.
- Wave-based rollout (pilot wave, then production waves ordered by criticality) to contain blast radius of any single wave.
- Standard subscription entitlement model appropriate to a disconnected environment (e.g., a local entitlement/content proxy); exact entitlement mechanism to be confirmed based on current environment connectivity model.

## Slide 6 — RHEL 9 Lifecycle & Support Benefits
- RHEL 9 carries a defined multi-phase vendor support lifecycle (Full Support, then Maintenance Support, with Extended Update/Life-cycle Support options in later phases) `[unverified — confirm exact phase end dates against the current Red Hat Enterprise Linux Life Cycle page before the support agreement is finalized]`.
- This replaces CentOS 7's community-only end-of-life situation with a vendor-accountable patch and errata process.
- Recommendation: capture the confirmed lifecycle dates in the final SOW so the customer's internal support planning aligns with actual vendor commitments.

## Slide 7 — Security & Compliance Approach
- The RFP requests "security certification coverage" without naming the specific standard. We do not assume a certification target; this is an open item requiring confirmation (e.g., which baseline — a CIS-style benchmark, an OpenSCAP profile, STIG, or another organization-specific standard).
- Baseline hardening included regardless of the final certification target: SELinux enforcing by default, crypto-policies set to an organization-approved level (FIPS mode only if formally required and confirmed `[unverified]`), audit logging enabled fleet-wide, and OpenSCAP-based configuration scanning against the confirmed baseline.
- Compliance is not automatic from having these mechanisms available — the specific control mappings must be validated once the target standard is confirmed.

## Slide 8 — Patch Management in a Disconnected Network
- All patch content is served from a local, internally hosted repository mirror; no host requires direct internet access to patch.
- The RFP also requests "real-time cloud-based operational insights," which directly conflicts with the "fully offline operation" requirement as literally stated.
- Proposed resolution: day-to-day patch and operations remain fully offline by default. If cloud-based insight capability is required, it must be scoped as a separate, explicitly reviewed exception (e.g., a scheduled, one-way, sanitized export on an approved cadence) rather than a standing live connection — this preserves the offline security posture while giving the customer a path to insight visibility if they choose to accept that scoped exception. Final decision belongs to the customer's security authority.

## Slide 9 — Operational Automation & Standard Build Process
- Standard build via Kickstart/Image Builder producing one golden image for all new and reimaged servers.
- Configuration enforced and drift-checked via Ansible content and RHEL system roles.
- Insights-driven proactive analysis is not available in a fully offline mode; where offline, drift and health reporting run against the local monitoring/logging stack instead `[flag as a known capability gap versus a connected deployment]`.

## Slide 10 — Business Value
- Reduced security and audit exposure from running an unsupported OS.
- Lower operational cost from one standardized build versus many inconsistent CentOS 7 builds.
- Predictable, vendor-backed support relationship replacing an unaccountable community-support model.

## Slide 11 — Operational Value & Service Continuity
- The RFP requests "zero service interruption" but does not state the current HA capability of in-scope workloads — this is the second open item.
- Where a workload already runs in an active/active or load-balanced HA configuration, rolling node-by-node migration can approach zero user-visible interruption.
- Where no HA exists today, migration for that workload requires a scheduled maintenance window; claiming zero interruption in that case would not be defensible. Recommend confirming HA status per application before the wave plan is finalized.

## Slide 12 — Risk & Mitigation
- Application incompatibility during upgrade — mitigated by pre-migration compatibility testing in a staging environment.
- Cutover/rollback risk — mitigated by full host snapshots/backups before each migration wave, with a defined rollback procedure per wave.
- Undefined certification and HA scope (Slides 7, 11) — mitigated by a confirmation workshop before final wave planning; treated as a schedule dependency, not a blocking delay.
- Schedule slip — mitigated by a contingency buffer in each migration phase.

## Slide 13 — Comparison with Community Rebuild Distributions
- Community rebuild distributions depend on upstream re-publishing cycles, which can introduce patch-availability lag versus a vendor-maintained release train `[unverified — confirm current rebuild vendor's published SLA, if any]`.
- They typically do not carry a single accountable vendor support relationship or a formal escalation path.
- RHEL 9 provides a direct, contracted vendor support and errata relationship, which is a material factor for a public-sector operator with formal accountability obligations. This is offered as the basis for comparison rather than a general superiority claim.

## Slide 14 — Support Model & Escalation
- Tiered escalation: local operations team (Tier 1) → certified support partner or vendor Tier 2 → vendor backline (Tier 3).
- Response-time targets per severity level to be defined in the support agreement (not yet confirmed `[unverified]`).
- Single named technical point of contact recommended for the engagement duration.

## Slide 15 — Migration Roadmap & Schedule
- Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4): Discovery, dependency mapping, certification/HA confirmation workshop, pilot wave (~10 servers).
- Phase 2 (Weeks 5-16): Wave-based production migration of the remaining ~190 servers, sequenced by criticality and confirmed HA status.
- Phase 3 (Weeks 17-20): Stabilization, validation, and CentOS 7 decommission.

## Slide 16 — Success Criteria, Conclusion & Next Steps
- Success criteria: 100% of estate on RHEL 9, zero unresolved P1 findings from post-migration validation, confirmed certification baseline applied, support SLAs signed.
- Conclusion: this proposal restores vendor-backed lifecycle and support coverage while keeping the environment inside its disconnected boundary; two scope items (certification target, HA status) remain open and are tracked as pre-planning actions, not blockers to starting the engagement.
- Next steps: confirm certification target and HA status, approve pilot wave, schedule kickoff workshop.
