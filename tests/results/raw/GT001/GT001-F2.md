# 16-Slide Proposal Blueprint: RHEL 9 Standardization (CentOS 7 Replacement)

## 1. Cover
Standardizing on RHEL 9 — A Supported Path Forward from CentOS 7
Prepared for a public-sector, disconnected-network operating environment.

## 2. Executive Summary
- The current estate (~200 servers) runs CentOS 7, which is past end of life and receives no further upstream security patches.
- This proposal standardizes the estate on RHEL 9 (assumed current minor release at contract time, e.g. 9.3/9.4 — exact minor to be confirmed `[unverified]`), migrated in controlled waves.
- Two items in the RFP are treated as open confirmation points rather than assumptions: the specific security certification target, and the existing HA capability behind the "zero interruption" requirement. Both are scoped explicitly rather than guessed at.

## 3. Customer Challenges
- CentOS 7 EOL removes the upstream security-patch safety net for ~200 production servers.
- No enforceable vendor SLA exists for the current OS base.
- Build inconsistency across the estate complicates both patch management and audit response.
- Primary audience: program/IT leadership evaluating business risk, and infrastructure/security staff evaluating technical fit.

## 4. Proposed Solution
- Standardize on RHEL 9 as the single supported enterprise Linux baseline.
- Consolidate to one golden build, one subscription/entitlement model, and one patch source.
- Preserve the existing disconnected-network operating model throughout migration and steady-state operations.

## 5. Migration Approach & Technical Architecture
- Full inventory and application-dependency assessment across the estate before wave planning.
- Leapp-based in-place upgrade where the source/target combination is supported; rebuild-and-restore otherwise `[unverified — confirm current Leapp support matrix against this estate's actual CentOS 7 minor versions]`.
- Four sequential migration waves, ordered from lowest to highest operational criticality.
- Local entitlement/content delivery mechanism suited to a disconnected environment (exact mechanism dependent on the customer's current connectivity model — to be confirmed during discovery).

## 6. RHEL 9 Lifecycle & Support Benefits
- RHEL 9 follows a defined multi-phase support lifecycle with vendor-issued errata, distinct from CentOS 7's community-only model `[specific phase end dates unverified — confirm against the current official lifecycle page before finalizing the support agreement]`.
- This gives the customer a contractual patch and support commitment in place of ad hoc community timelines.
- Recommend the confirmed lifecycle dates be written directly into the SOW rather than referenced informally.

## 7. Security & Compliance Approach
- "Security certification coverage" is not named in the RFP. Rather than assuming a target, we propose a short confirmation step to identify the applicable baseline (organization-specific standard, an OpenSCAP profile, STIG, or similar) before hardening design is finalized.
- Regardless of the final target, the baseline build includes SELinux enforcing, an approved crypto-policy level, and centralized audit logging; FIPS mode is included only if formally required and confirmed with the customer `[unverified]`.
- We are explicit that having these controls available does not by itself satisfy any named certification — mapping to the confirmed standard is a required follow-on step.

## 8. Patch Management in a Disconnected Network
- Patch content is delivered exclusively from an internally hosted repository mirror; hosts do not reach the internet directly.
- This creates a direct tension with the RFP's separate request for "real-time cloud-based operational insights" — the two requirements cannot both be satisfied literally at once.
- Proposed handling: keep the environment fully offline as the default operating mode. Treat any cloud-insight capability as an optional, separately scoped and separately security-reviewed capability (for example, a scheduled batch export rather than a persistent connection), to be enabled only if the customer's security authority explicitly approves it. We do not recommend silently blending the two requirements without that approval.

## 9. Operational Automation & Standard Build Process
- Kickstart/Image Builder-based standard build applied to every new or rebuilt server.
- RHEL system roles and configuration management enforce and re-assert the golden configuration.
- Because standard vendor telemetry/insight services assume connectivity, offline operation means health and drift reporting rely on the local monitoring stack instead — this is a known trade-off of the offline posture, not a gap to be hidden.

## 10. Business Value
- Removes the security and compliance liability of an unsupported OS.
- Converts inconsistent, hard-to-audit builds into one standardized, reportable baseline.
- Replaces an unaccountable support model with a contracted vendor relationship.

## 11. Operational Value & Service Continuity
- The RFP's "zero service interruption" requirement is not paired with a stated HA capability for the affected workloads — this is the second open item.
- For workloads already deployed with load-balanced or active/active HA, rolling per-node migration can come close to zero user-visible impact.
- For workloads without existing HA, a scheduled maintenance window is required, and describing that migration as "zero interruption" would not be accurate. We recommend confirming HA status per workload before committing to a per-application continuity claim.

## 12. Risk & Mitigation
- Compatibility risk: mitigated through pre-migration testing in a non-production environment before each wave.
- Cutover risk: mitigated through pre-wave backups/snapshots and a documented rollback procedure.
- Scope ambiguity (certification target, HA status): tracked as a discovery-phase confirmation item rather than left implicit.
- Schedule risk: mitigated with contingency time built into each wave.

## 13. Comparison with Community Rebuild Distributions
- Community rebuild distributions rely on upstream re-publishing cadence, which can lag a vendor-maintained release train `[unverified — confirm current comparator's published patch cadence, if published]`.
- They generally do not carry a formal, contracted vendor support and escalation relationship.
- The comparison here is limited to vendor support/escalation, lifecycle predictability, and certification-ecosystem access — not a general quality claim — because those are the criteria that matter for a public-sector operator with formal accountability requirements.

## 14. Support Model & Escalation
- Tiered support: local Tier 1 operations, escalating to a certified partner or vendor Tier 2, then vendor backline Tier 3.
- Severity-based response targets to be defined in the support agreement (not yet set `[unverified]`).
- A single named technical contact is recommended for the duration of the engagement.

## 15. Migration Roadmap & Schedule
- Weeks 1-3: Discovery, dependency mapping, and the certification/HA confirmation session.
- Weeks 4-15: Wave-based migration of the remaining estate.
- Weeks 16-18: Stabilization, validation, and CentOS 7 decommission.

## 16. Success Criteria, Conclusion & Next Steps
- Success criteria: full estate migrated to RHEL 9, confirmed certification baseline applied and validated, support agreement signed with defined SLAs, no unresolved critical findings post-migration.
- Conclusion: this proposal restores a vendor-supported lifecycle for the estate while preserving the disconnected operating model; the certification target and HA status remain open items to confirm during discovery, not blockers to starting the engagement.
- Next steps: schedule the certification/HA confirmation session, approve the pilot wave, and finalize the support agreement.
